Microlearning - is it the future of Learning?

Learning is a lifelong activity. People do not stop learning after they earn their diplomas. Unlike high school or college courses however, people have more control over how, when, and what they learn after graduation.

In some cases, courses can actually be done in a matter of hours. These days, experts have found courses designed for microlearning to be quite effective in helping learners acquire new skills and develop habits.

For those who are employed or are preparing to get employed, additional training programs are necessary to ensure that they are properly equipped for the demands of their jobs. The need to upgrade their skills and knowledge is apparent.

What might not be too apparent is that it’s not only in the corporate setting that people need to upgrade themselves. Learning can be elective as part of one’s desire to develop himself.

He can choose to learn anything from cooking and crafting to high tech coding and programming. With microlearning, people can effectively learn new things on their own without having to attend conventional corporate training sessions or enrolling in formal school-based classes.

What is Microlearning ?

In simple terms, micro learning can be defined as way of learning wherein the lessons are limited to short and concise sessions. The time factor and the scope of learning are not the only considerations.

However, Three key elements should be present:

  • Performance Objective – the focus of each session should be clear and a concrete learning outcome should be specified at the onset. The learner should know exactly what performance improvement to expect after he has completed the session.
  • Single Learning Activity – the session or lesson should be delivered using only one type of activity (i.e. game, video, Q&A, simulation, etc.) that tackles the given concept. Choosing which type of activity to use in the session would depend on the given topic as well as the target students.
  • Broader Learning Strategy – although micro learning sessions can stand on their own, they should be part of a broader strategy that is composed of other sessions covering similar or related skills and concepts.

The use of the micro learning technique is now widely accepted in online training and mobile learning. Many are offered as subscription services wherein students or learners can simply access whatever session they wish to take at any given time. Gamification has also increased the attractiveness as well as the effectiveness of these online courses.

Whether for personal development or professional growth, there is no denying that micro learning is one of the most effective and widely used methods of digital education nowadays. This has been proven both in scientific studies and in real life experiences.

Conventional Learning vs Microlearning

In broad terms, people learn through systematic education and day-to-day experiential education. Systematic education refers to formal education wherein learners attend class in institutions of learning or in training venues. Learning is highly structured and performance measures are set towards the goal of awarding a degree or a certification to those who successfully complete the courses.

Day-to-day experiential education involves an informal type of learning wherein people pick up information, knowledge, and even skills from their day-to-day experiences and interactions with other people.

Such learning happens in a non-structured manner and perhaps not intentional as well. Learning in this sense often depends on factors like influence, exposure, and interest. This type of learning does not often result in any performance evaluation and grading or in any professional qualification.

Another type of conventional learning is non-formal education. This comes by way of training sessions that mostly focus on skills acquisition and development. Apprenticeships and mentoring fall under this category of learning. Through this mode of education, people learn through practice.

Microlearning takes various elements from all these three types of education. Although this method of learning can also be applied to formal, non-formal, and informal learners, it actually takes on a form of its own when applied to adult learners.

Compared to conventional types of learning, micro courses are elective, more learner-centric, and flexible. This type of learning supports lifelong and continuous learning – something that is considered to be a necessity for personal fulfillment and success.

Microlearning in Science

Scientific studies have proven that the brain’s absorption and retention of information and data is limited. It is thus sensible for micro learning to expose the brain to small chunks of information in shorter periods of time. This is comparable to the chunking concept of George Miller, a psychologist.

Another scientist also put forth a theory about how the human brain retains the information it is exposed to.

In research studies by German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus, results showed a common forgetting curve among learners where information learned is “forgotten” exponentially over time. Within a few days of exposure to the learning materials, retention drops to about 40% and then further down to 21% in a month’s time.

The human memory has also been affected by available technology today. Experts believe that memory is a skill that needs to be practiced to remain sharp.

With memory tools and data storage devices available today, people have become more dependent on these external banks rather than their own memory. The lack of use weakens the memory.

These researches and the way technology has affected how the human brain retains information have spurred the development of using micro courses in learning and education.

Benefits and Disadvantages of Microlearning

“Quick and easy” is perhaps one phrase that would apply to learning through micro courses. There is more to this method of learning, however, than the scope and duration of each module.

As there are advantages that can be gained, there are also downsides to this method. Briefly, here are some of the benefits and disadvantages of micro courses:

Benefits

  • Rich media format delivery – snippets of information are delivered in rich media formats, making this type of learning more engaging and exciting for the learners. The lessons are delivered in the media that’s deemed to be more effective in helping the learners absorb the information within a short period of time.
  • Learner-centric – this type of learning takes the learners needs and preferences into consideration in the design of the short-burst modules. Learners get the specific lessons that they need and are interested in without having to wait for class schedules or for an instructor.
  • Just-in-time availability – the lessons are available just at the right time when the learner needs or wants them. This saves time and effort for the learners as they get the information they require in a convenient manner. At the same time, the desired knowledge and skills are gained almost immediately so the learners can apply them right away.
  • Multiple-device access – anytime, anywhere learning is made possible when the elearning modules are available and accessible not only through desktops and laptops but through mobile devices such as smartphones and laptops. In fact, quick smartphone games and 2- to 3-minute YouTube videos are popular and effective formats for microlearning modules.

Disadvantages

  • Fragmented learning – because the lessons are given in short bursts, learners might have difficulties in linking the ideas to each other. In the case of personal development, learners could even choose to skip modules or only take those micro modules that they are interested in.
  • Uncertainty of synthesis – micro courses are usually taken solo or on an individual basis. There is no instructor or mentor to ensure that the learner completely understood all the modules and is able to synthesize all the information to achieve his learning and performance goals.
  • Differences in learner reception and understanding – not everyone learns the same way and not every lesson can effectively be learned in short bursts. The results of micro learning depends on whether the micro courses are designed to match the student’s learning style and if the subject matter can be broken down and encapsulated successfully.

With proper design and administration of micro courses, the downsides of this method of learning can be addressed to maximize the benefits.

Experts in micro learning should be able to devise ways to mitigate these issues through additional content like overviews, diagnostics, and course summaries.

Examples of Microlearning

The beauty of this learning method is the flexibility and versatility that it offers course developers and educators. They can actually make the modules more exciting and thus easier to absorb and retain by the learners. Here are some examples of how creative the modules can be:

1. Game apps – educators can use short games that can be played on mobile devices like tablets and smartphone. One such app is MinecraftEdu.

Learners of different ages and grade levels can choose the games that cover the topics and subject matters they need to study. Specific learning objectives are set to be met at the end of the game. The app also features games that allow teacher-student interaction as well as partner or group collaboration.

2. Quizzes – this is another popular interactive online tool for elearning. Quizzes are not only limited for use as diagnostics. They can be used to stimulate critical thinking and open the mind for new information.

A quick 5- item quiz with each question followed by a short discussion of the correct answer would be a good example of a microlearning module.

Duolingo, a language learning app, also makes use of quizzes to effectively measure performance. As these quizzes are graded right away, learners get feedback on what areas they need to work on more.

Through this app, those who wish to learn a new language can take short lessons that cover speaking and listening as well as translating. After these short lessons, they can take quizzes and earn points in the process.

3. Flashcards – there is nothing new about the use of flashcards in learning and retaining new information. In micro courses, however, flashcards take on a digital form.

There are programs like Flashcard Learner that can be downloaded by users. Some of these programs already have built in lessons, while there are also those that users can customize based on what they wish to learn.

4. Gamification – yet another exciting way to learn and relax at the same time. As opposed to simply playing a game app, a gamified elearning online tool would immerse the learner in the concepts he should learn and allows him to practice them in a safe and risk-free virtual environment.

In the process, he is rewarded with points that earn him the recognition of climbing up the leaderboards and being awarded with badges.

Nike’s Nike+ apps are examples of gamified ways of learning. In this case, the microlearning focuses on health and fitness concepts and rewards the users for constant and consistent activity.

5. Blended learning – this makes use of the combination of micro courses and traditional in-classroom learning methodologies. While the student maintains control over when and where he gets his modules, this application of the microlearning method of learning puts a teacher or an instructor into the picture.

Instead of live lectures, blended learning features instructional videos. The instructor serves to facilitate structured activities in-classroom and to guide the students in problem solving using the new concepts they learned in the micro courses.

6. Video tutorials and instructional – these combine the learning experience with entertainment. That’s why “How-to” videos on YouTube are very popular. When anyone needs a quick less on how to do virtually anything under the sky, he can simply do an online search and find a video to walk him through the entire process.

The beauty about these videos is that they often skip through all the technical jargon and actually shows you exactly how things are done step by step.

Oreo’s simple illustrated videos, for instance, show their audience how to make delicious goodies and treats using easy to follow recipes. These videos run for under 25 seconds, yet they effectively teach their audience something new.

To supplement the videos, links to the recipes are provided as well so that the viewers can try the whole process on their own or perhaps make the recipes as they view the video.

Summary

One piece at a time, of course. The same thing applies to learning. You cannot possible digest a huge body of information in one sitting. You need to divide it into bite-sized pieces that are easy for you to wrap your mind around.

That’s what microlearning is all about. It’s scientifically proven to be effective. At the same time, it’s an even more exciting and engaging way to learn new things on your own and upgrade your knowledge and skills.

There is really no limit to learning and education. Limitations like age, time, and resources can successfully be hurdled by those who thirst for new learning. Continuously upgrading yourself is not impossible to do if you truly desire it.
